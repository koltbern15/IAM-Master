# Hybrid Identity

Most real enterprises live in the space between AD DS and Entra ID. They have 20 years of on-prem infrastructure, a forest full of users and devices, and a Microsoft 365 deployment that predates any serious identity governance conversation. The bridge between those two worlds — the plumbing that makes one user account work in both — is hybrid identity. Getting this architecture right is one of the most consequential infrastructure decisions a Microsoft-focused enterprise will make in this decade.

## What it is

Hybrid identity is the model in which an on-premises Active Directory Domain Services forest is the <Definition term="authoritative source of truth">The system whose identity data is treated as canonical. In a hybrid identity deployment, AD DS is typically the authoritative source: users are created, modified, and disabled in AD DS first. Entra Connect (formerly AAD Connect) replicates those changes to Entra ID. Changes made directly to cloud-only attributes in Entra ID are permitted but changes to synchronized attributes (displayName, UPN, password hash) must flow from AD DS, or they will be overwritten on the next sync cycle.</Definition> for user identities, and Entra ID serves as the cloud-facing authentication layer for SaaS applications and Microsoft 365 workloads.

<Definition term="Entra Connect (AAD Connect)">Microsoft's synchronization engine that runs on-prem (on a domain-joined Windows Server) and replicates identity objects from AD DS to Entra ID on a ~30-minute cycle by default. It handles user account sync, group sync, device sync, and — depending on the sign-in method chosen — password hash synchronization. Entra Connect is the mechanical heart of every hybrid identity deployment. Its replacement, Cloud Sync, runs as an Entra ID-managed lightweight agent rather than a full Windows Server application.</Definition>

There are three sign-in methods. The choice between them determines where authentication happens, which system owns the credential, and what your blast radius is if something gets compromised.

## Why it exists

Pure cloud (Entra-only, no AD DS) is the clean architectural ideal. It is also the rarest deployment pattern in practice. Most enterprises have accumulated decades of on-prem infrastructure — applications that speak Kerberos and LDAP, printers that authenticate against AD DS, legacy LOB systems that the vendor explicitly requires be integrated with an on-prem domain, and organizational muscle memory built around AD DS admin tooling.

The alternative to hybrid identity is a forklift migration: lift every application off AD DS dependency simultaneously, move every user account to cloud-only, and flip the switch. In practice this is a 3-7 year program for a large organization, not an event. Hybrid identity is the architecture that lets you make incremental progress — migrating application by application toward cloud authentication while the on-prem world continues to function — rather than betting everything on a big-bang cutover.

<ProTip>
The "hybrid" designation is often temporary in aspiration even when permanent in practice. Most organizations that began hybrid identity deployments in 2018-2020 are still hybrid in 2026, usually because of the same small set of blockers: a legacy application with a hard Kerberos dependency, a vendor who cannot be persuaded to update their SAML configuration, or an organizational decision to keep AD DS as the HR source-of-truth for provisioning. Plan for hybrid to be durable infrastructure. Do not architecture as though you will finish migrating to cloud-only "by Q3."
</ProTip>

## How it works under the hood

### Password Hash Synchronization (PHS)

PHS is the method Microsoft recommends for most organizations, and it is the correct default choice unless you have a specific documented requirement for something else.

The mechanism is a chain of derivations, and getting the chain right is what separates a correct mental model from a hand-wave. AD DS does not store your plaintext password — it stores the **MD4 hash** of it (the NTLM hash, held in the `unicodePwd` attribute). Entra Connect never sees the plaintext. What it does is take that 16-byte MD4 hash and run a second, deliberately expensive derivation over it: it converts the MD4 hash to a 32-character hexadecimal string, adds a per-user random salt, and feeds salt + hash through **PBKDF2 with HMAC-SHA256 and 1,000 iterations**. The output of that PBKDF2 pass is the value shipped to Entra ID — roughly every 2 minutes. So the synced credential is *SHA256-based PBKDF2 over the MD4 hash*, two hashing layers deep, never the plaintext and never the bare MD4 hash on its own. The original MD4 hash never leaves your network, and the synced derivative cannot be reversed to recover the MD4 hash or the plaintext password — but it can still be used to validate the same password, because the same plaintext run through the same chain produces the same result.

The consequential characteristic of PHS: **authentication happens in Entra ID**. The user's credentials are validated against the synced hash in the cloud. This means AD DS does not need to be reachable for authentication to succeed. If your VPN goes down, if your DC has a storage failure, if half your data center loses power — users can still authenticate to Microsoft 365 and Entra-integrated SaaS apps. This outage resilience is the dominant reason PHS wins most PHS-vs-PTA decisions in practice.

PHS also enables **Entra ID Identity Protection's leaked credential detection**. Microsoft can compare the hashed derivatives against known-compromised credential databases and alert when a match is found — a capability that requires Entra ID to hold the hash derivative. PTA and Federation cannot enable this feature.

The objection most often raised against PHS: "We can't let our passwords leave the building." This objection is technically imprecise (the NT hash, not the password, is being derived and synced — and even the NT hash is not what's synced), but organizationally real. In high-assurance environments where any credential material leaving the network perimeter triggers a compliance finding, PTA is the answer.

### Pass-Through Authentication (PTA)

PTA keeps the credential validation on-prem. A lightweight PTA Agent runs on one or more domain-joined servers in your environment. When a user signs in to Entra ID, Entra ID does not validate the password itself — instead it encrypts the credentials with a public key, places the request on an Azure Service Bus queue, and the on-prem PTA Agent pulls the request, decrypts it with its private key, and calls `LogonUser()` against the local domain controller. The DC returns pass or fail; the PTA Agent relays the result back through the service bus to Entra ID, which issues or denies the token.

What PTA provides that PHS does not: **on-prem AD DS owns every authentication decision in real time**. If a user account is disabled in AD DS, the next PTA authentication returns failure immediately — there is no window between when the account is disabled and when Entra ID knows about it. With PHS, the disabled account state syncs within ~30 minutes; during that window, a user with a recently disabled account could theoretically authenticate against the cached hash in Entra ID (Entra Connect handles this via the `accountDisabled` sync, but the timing is looser than PTA).

What PTA does not provide: outage resilience. If on-prem AD DS is unavailable (DC failure, network outage), PTA authentication fails. Users cannot sign in to Microsoft 365 even though the cloud service is fully operational. This is often the argument that breaks PTA adoption in organizations that have experienced extended DC outages. You can mitigate this with multiple PTA agents across multiple sites, but the fundamental on-prem dependency remains.

PTA also does not enable leaked credential detection from Identity Protection for the same reason PHS does — Entra ID does not hold any credential material to compare against breach databases.

### Federation (ADFS)

Federation is the legacy architecture that organizations adopted before PHS and PTA existed, and that Microsoft has been actively deprecating since 2019.

In federated mode, Entra ID does not validate credentials at all. When Entra ID sees a sign-in attempt for a federated domain, it performs Home Realm Discovery (HRD), determines that the UPN suffix is federated, and redirects the browser to the on-prem ADFS endpoint. ADFS validates the user against AD DS via Windows Integrated Authentication (Kerberos + NTLM), mints a SAML 2.0 assertion with the user's claims, and posts it back to Entra ID. Entra ID trusts the ADFS assertion (because you configured a federated trust when you ran `Convert-MsolDomainToFederated`) and issues its own Entra ID tokens to the user.

Why Federation became dangerous: the federated trust model means Entra ID's security is contingent on ADFS's security. If an attacker compromises your ADFS server — gains code execution, steals the token signing certificate — they can mint SAML tokens for **any user in your Entra ID tenant**, including Global Administrators, with no on-prem credential required. The forged token passes Entra ID's signature verification because it's signed with the real ADFS signing certificate.

This is not a theoretical attack. It is exactly what happened in the **Solorigate / SolarWinds breach (2020)**. The threat actor (Cozy Bear / APT29) gained access to ADFS infrastructure at dozens of organizations and used forged SAML tokens to access Microsoft 365 data, persistence tooling, and email with no additional authentication required. The breach disclosure caused Microsoft to significantly accelerate its PHS + Seamless SSO recommendation and introduce a new class of Identity Protection detections specifically for anomalous SAML token activity.

ADFS is now formally in maintenance mode. Microsoft ships security patches; no new features are planned. The official guidance is to migrate all federated domains to PHS + Seamless SSO.

### Seamless SSO — the desktop trick

Seamless SSO is a feature that works alongside either PHS or PTA to provide single sign-on from domain-joined Windows devices without requiring users to re-enter credentials. The mechanism: Entra Connect creates a computer account in AD DS named `AZUREADSSOACC`. Kerberos is configured to issue Kerberos tickets to this account for users signing in from domain-joined machines. When a user opens a browser on a domain-joined PC and navigates to a Microsoft 365 sign-in, Entra ID asks the browser to present a Kerberos ticket for `AZUREADSSOACC`. The browser does so silently via Windows Integrated Authentication. Entra ID validates the ticket and issues tokens without the user seeing a sign-in prompt.

The security implication: `AZUREADSSOACC`'s Kerberos keys are the trust anchor for Seamless SSO. The keys must be rotated regularly (Microsoft recommends every 30 days) to limit the blast radius if the account is compromised. Failure to rotate leaves you with a permanent, high-value target in AD DS — a computer account whose compromise yields silent SSO for any domain user to Microsoft 365.

### Cloud Sync vs. Connect Sync

Microsoft has introduced a new synchronization architecture alongside the original Entra Connect: **Entra Cloud Sync**. Cloud Sync replaces the heavyweight Windows Server application (Entra Connect requires a dedicated VM running .NET 4.x and has complex installation requirements) with a lightweight provisioning agent that communicates outbound to the Entra ID service. The sync configuration lives in the Entra portal rather than on-prem tooling.

Cloud Sync is the forward-looking architecture. It supports multi-forest synchronization without complex metadirectory configuration, has a simpler deployment footprint, and receives new feature investment. The current gap: Cloud Sync does not yet support **all** the features of Connect Sync. As of 2026, key Connect-only features include: device writeback (required for Hybrid Entra Join), Exchange hybrid writeback (for Exchange on-prem mailbox attributes), and some custom attribute flow scenarios. For organizations that need those capabilities, Connect Sync remains required.

The migration path: deploy Cloud Sync for new forests or for scopes where Connect Sync's gaps are not blockers. Plan to migrate existing Connect Sync deployments to Cloud Sync as the feature parity gap closes — Microsoft's roadmap signals Cloud Sync will achieve full parity and Connect Sync will enter deprecation, though no end-of-life date has been announced as of this writing.

## War story

<WarStory title="The 18-Month ADFS Migration Nobody Wanted to Do">
A community bank — about 2,200 employees, 45 branches — had been running ADFS 3.0 (Windows Server 2012 R2) since 2014. The original architect had read a Microsoft whitepaper arguing that "regulated industries require federation" and had built the federated trust before PHS was even a supported option. By the time federation was questioned, it had been in place for six years and nobody alive on the team had been there to build it.

Solorigate broke in December 2020. Within two weeks of the public disclosure, every security team in financial services was having the same conversation: what is our ADFS blast radius? At this bank, the answer was stark. The ADFS servers were running on hardware that hadn't been patched since the previous year's maintenance window. The token signing certificate had never been rotated. The ADFS farm was accessible from the general employee VLAN — no firewall rule restricted it to the DMZ. If an attacker reached the ADFS server with code execution, they owned the Entra ID tenant.

The CISO decided: migrate off ADFS. The target: PHS + Seamless SSO. Estimated timeline: 6 months. Actual duration: 18 months.

The first eight months went as planned — Entra Connect deployed, password hashes syncing, `AZUREADSSOACC` created, Seamless SSO working for the majority of the user population. The converted domain validated against a test group of 50 users. No issues.

Month nine: the project hit the first surprise. The bank's internet banking platform used a home-grown SAML integration that was hardcoded to the ADFS `entityID` (`https://adfs.bankname.com/adfs/services/trust`). The vendor who built the integration had gone out of business. The SAML metadata was configured on the bank's internet banking server with no fallback. Converting the domain to PHS broke internet banking for every external customer the moment the federated domain was converted.

The remediation required engaging a third-party middleware vendor to reconfigure the SAML SP to trust Entra ID's SAML endpoint directly. That took three months and a meaningful contract negotiation.

Month fourteen: a second surprise. A critical third-party HR application used ADFS for its SCIM provisioning connector — the provisioning logic queried ADFS for group membership claims to determine which employees got access to which HR modules. The documentation said "SAML IdP integration"; nowhere did it mention the hardcoded claim mapping against ADFS-specific attributes. Another two months of vendor engagement.

Month eighteen: all federated domains converted. ADFS servers decommissioned. The ADFS retirement runbook took two days to execute cleanly.

The lessons: federation creates an invisible web of dependencies that are invisible until you try to remove them. Before beginning an ADFS-to-PHS migration, run a discovery exercise specifically looking for SAML Service Provider integrations, ADFS claim rules used by downstream applications, and any hardcoded endpoint references to your ADFS `entityID` or ADFS hostname. That inventory will surface the surprises before they block the project.

In a SaaS-first organization, federation is rarely the architecture — Entra-only or Okta-brokered SAML is more common, and the ADFS migration problem doesn't apply. In defense environments, ADFS is still common where the classified AD DS forest is the air-gapped source of truth and federation is the only practical way to produce tokens for on-prem apps without syncing hashes off-network — a legitimate use case that PHS cannot serve in a disconnected environment. In healthcare, the canonical nightmare is the organization running ADFS + Epic + on-prem AD DS where Epic has been integrated against the ADFS SAML endpoint by the Epic implementation team and the Epic contract specifies that changes to the authentication architecture require a paid engagement. Challenge that contract before you commit to an ADFS migration timeline.
</WarStory>
