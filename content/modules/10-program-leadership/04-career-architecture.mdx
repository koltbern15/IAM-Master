# IAM Career Architecture

The first nine modules taught you the craft. This one is about the career you build with it. Where the rest of the program makes you better at *doing* IAM, this section is about deciding where you are going, proving you are getting there, and — for you specifically — turning the frameworks you have just learned into a business.

You are sitting on an unusual combination: hands-on IAM at a regulated community bank, a military background that opens veteran-contracting doors, and a curriculum's worth of repeatable frameworks. Most people have one of those. This section is the scaffolding for stacking all three into a deliberate path instead of a series of accidents.

## The IAM Career Ladder

<HoloPanel label="THE LADDER — TWO TRACKS, ONE INFLECTION">

IAM careers run on two parallel tracks, and the most important decision is not how high you climb but which track you climb.

**Individual-contributor (IC) track** — depth and technical authority:

- **IAM Analyst** — runs access requests, joiner-mover-leaver tasks, and reviews.
- **IAM Engineer** — builds and operates the tooling: connectors, automation, MFA, federation.
- **Senior IAM Engineer** — owns hard integrations and mentors; the technical go-to.
- **IAM / Identity Architect** — designs the target-state architecture across the estate.
- **Principal / Distinguished Engineer** — sets technical direction org-wide.

**Leadership track** — scope and program ownership:

- **IAM Team Lead** — coordinates the work and the people doing it.
- **IAM Manager** — owns delivery, budget input, and headcount.
- **Director of IAM** — owns the program, its strategy, and its budget.
- **CISO-adjacent / CISO** — owns security risk at the executive table.

The inflection — the move that actually changes your career — is the shift from **doing the tickets to owning the program**. An analyst's success is measured in tasks closed; a program owner's is measured in risk reduced and roadmaps delivered. Everything in Modules 01 through 03 of *this* module (maturity assessment, business cases, metrics, RFPs) is the language of the program-owner side of the ladder. Learning it is how you signal you are ready to cross the line.

Compensation tracks scope and track, and bands vary widely by region, employer, and clearance — confirm current market figures against live salary data for your geography before you anchor a negotiation on a number.

</HoloPanel>

<ProTip>
You do not have to pick IC or leadership forever, but you should know which one you are climbing *right now*. The two tracks reward different work: the IC track rewards going deeper on the technology, the leadership track rewards going wider on people, money, and risk. Trying to advance on both at once usually means advancing on neither. Decide your next two years, not your whole life.
</ProTip>

## Certification Stacking Strategy

<HoloPanel label="CERT STACK — EACH RUNG SIGNALS A LAYER">

Certifications are signals, and a good stack signals an *arc*, not a pile. Your actual path — SC-900 → SC-300 → Security+ → CISSP — is a deliberate progression, and it is worth understanding what each rung says to a reader:

- **SC-900 (Microsoft Security, Compliance & Identity Fundamentals)** — signals you speak the Microsoft security vocabulary. Entry layer.
- **SC-300 (Microsoft Identity and Access Administrator)** — signals hands-on Entra ID / identity-admin capability. This is your domain credential.
- **Security+ (CompTIA SY0-701)** — signals the broad security baseline. It matters doubly for the Guard: it has long been the common baseline certification for the DoD's IAT Level II workforce role. Note that the DoD has moved from the old **8570** directive to **DoD Directive 8140** and the **DoDM 8140.03** qualification model, which frames the workforce around DoD Cyber Workforce Framework (DCWF) work roles rather than the legacy IAT/IAM category labels; the IAT II language still appears in postings and contracts, but confirm the current 8140 qualification options for your specific work role before relying on it.
- **CISSP (ISC2)** — signals management and architecture credibility. This is the consulting door-opener and the credential that reads as "can own a program," not just operate a tool.

**Adjacent stacking options**, once the core path is done:

- **Microsoft SC-100** — cybersecurity architect; the natural step past SC-300 on the Microsoft track.
- **Vendor IAM certs** (Okta, SailPoint, CyberArk, and similar) — delivery and sales-channel credibility for consulting; a partner cert can open a partner channel.
- **Cloud certs** (AWS, Azure, GCP) with an IAM-adjacent focus — depth where the identity work increasingly lives.

Confirm current exam codes and the DoD-approved qualification list against the official sources before committing money or a timeline — exam codes and the 8140 qualification matrices change.

</HoloPanel>

## Consulting Positioning: Your Frameworks Are Your Products

<HoloPanel label="PRODUCTIZE — THE FRAMEWORKS ARE THE OFFERING">

Here is the move that connects this whole module to your consulting ambition: the frameworks you just built in Modules 01 through 03 are not study aids — they are **billable deliverables waiting to be packaged**.

- The **maturity self-assessment** from Section 01 becomes a fixed-scope *IAM Maturity Assessment* engagement.
- The **roadmap table** becomes a *Roadmap & Sequencing* deliverable.
- The **business-case skeleton** from Section 02 becomes *Business-Case Development*.
- The **RFP requirements and scoring matrix** from Section 03 become *RFP Design & Vendor-Scoring Facilitation*.
- An access-review program stand-up becomes a recurring *Access-Review Program* engagement.

A consulting practice is repeatable deliverables sold into a defensible niche. Yours is sitting right in front of you: **regulated / community-bank IAM** (you live the FFIEC examiner reality every day) crossed with **federal / veteran-owned positioning** (the Guard background and the VOSB/SDVOSB path). That intersection is narrow enough to own and broad enough to feed a business.

The artifact that markets this is the **capability statement** — the one-page document a government or enterprise buyer expects before they will talk to you. It anchors your competencies to the niche and lists the codes and certifications that make you a real, findable vendor.

</HoloPanel>

<Definition term="capability statement">A standardized one-page government-contracting marketing document that summarizes a small business for a prospective buyer or contracting officer. The standard sections are a company overview, core competencies, differentiators, past performance, and the identifying codes and certifications (NAICS codes, CAGE code, UEI, and any set-aside designations such as SDVOSB), plus contact information. It is the front-door artifact a federal buyer expects to see before engaging.</Definition>

<ProTip>
Do not give the frameworks away to "build goodwill" and hope for referrals, and do not sell them as raw downloadable templates. The value is not the template — it is *you facilitating the engagement* and interpreting the result. Package each framework as a fixed-scope deliverable with your expertise attached. The template is the artifact; the judgment is the product.
</ProTip>

## VOSB / SDVOSB Positioning

<HoloPanel label="VETERAN CONTRACTING — KNOW THE RULES, THEN CONFIRM THEM">

For a veteran building a consulting practice, the veteran-contracting designations are a real competitive lever — and the rules changed recently enough that you must verify every specific before you rely on it.

- **VOSB** is a Veteran-Owned Small Business; **SDVOSB** is a Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business, where the controlling owner has a service-connected disability rating. The SDVOSB designation is the one that unlocks federal set-aside and sole-source eligibility.
- **Certification now runs through the SBA.** Effective **January 1, 2023**, certification of VOSBs and SDVOSBs moved from the VA's Center for Verification and Evaluation (CVE) to the **SBA's Veteran Small Business Certification (VetCert)** program. A consequence of that move: for non-VA federal procurements, businesses can no longer simply self-certify as SDVOSB — certification through SBA VetCert is required to compete for SDVOSB set-asides.
- **There is a government-wide contracting goal.** The federal government has long carried a statutory goal of awarding at least **3%** of contracting dollars to SDVOSBs. More recent National Defense Authorization Act language has moved to raise that government-wide goal (the FY2024 NDAA increased the target above the historic 3% floor) — confirm the current applicable percentage before quoting it in marketing.
- **SAM.gov is the front door.** Active registration in SAM.gov is the baseline for doing business with the federal government; your VetCert status and codes live there.

Treat every percentage, eligibility rule, and program name here as something to confirm against the live SBA, SAM.gov, and VA sources before it goes into a proposal — these rules are litigated and revised, and an out-of-date claim in a capability statement is worse than no claim.

</HoloPanel>

<Definition term="SDVOSB">Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business — a small business that is at least 51% owned and controlled by one or more veterans with a service-connected disability rating. Federal SDVOSB certification is handled through the SBA's Veteran Small Business Certification (VetCert) program (moved from the VA's CVE effective January 1, 2023), and the designation unlocks eligibility for federal SDVOSB set-aside and sole-source contracts. Confirm current eligibility and ownership-control rules against SBA before relying on them.</Definition>
