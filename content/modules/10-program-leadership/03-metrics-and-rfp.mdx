# Metrics &amp; RFP Design

You cannot run a program you cannot measure, and you cannot buy a tool you cannot score. This section gives you two reusable consulting artifacts: a metrics catalog plus a one-slide executive dashboard, and an RFP requirements template plus a weighted scoring matrix. Everything here is a starting point. The tables are seeded with examples and placeholders. Your job is to delete what does not fit your environment, fill in the targets that match your real risk appetite, and turn the scaffolding into something you would actually put in front of your board or a client.

A note before you start: every number in here that looks specific (a 24-hour deprovisioning SLA, a target percentage) is illustrative, not a standard. Treat them as prompts, not facts. Replace them with the numbers you can defend.

## Metrics, KPIs, and KRIs are not the same thing <SC300Badge />

The fastest way to lose a board's attention is to report the wrong kind of number to the wrong audience. Three distinct things get lumped together as "metrics," and keeping them straight is what makes your reporting land.

<Definition term="key risk indicator (KRI)">A leading indicator that risk exposure is rising *before* an incident occurs. Unlike a KPI (which measures how you performed against a target after the fact), a KRI is meant to give you early warning. Example: a rising count of legacy-authentication sign-ins is a KRI for credential-stuffing exposure — it tells you the attack surface is growing before anyone is breached.</Definition>

- **Operational metric** — a raw count or measurement. "We have 1,420 active accounts." True, but it answers no question on its own. This is the data layer; it feeds the other two.
- **KPI (Key Performance Indicator)** — performance measured against a target. "94% of terminations were deprovisioned within SLA, against a 98% target." This is lagging and outcome-focused. It tells you whether the program is doing its job.
- **KRI (Key Risk Indicator)** — a leading signal that risk is rising. "Legacy-auth sign-ins are up 30% quarter over quarter." This is forward-looking. It tells you where the next problem is forming.

The audience determines which you report. Your IT operations team lives in operational metrics. Your CISO and risk committee want KPIs (are we meeting our commitments?) and KRIs (where is risk trending?). The board wants a tiny handful of KRIs translated into business risk, plus the trend line and the ask.

<ProTip>
Report trend and risk, not telemetry. A dashboard that says "MFA coverage rose from 82% to 91% this quarter, closing the gap on our highest-residual-risk control" tells a story. A dashboard that dumps 40 raw counts from your IdP says "I do not know what matters." The executive version is storytelling backed by evidence, not a data export.
</ProTip>

<Definition term="segregation of duties (SoD)">A control principle requiring that no single person holds a combination of access that would let them both commit and conceal an error or fraud (for example, the ability to both create a vendor and approve payment to it). In IAM, SoD shows up as toxic-combination policies enforced at access-request time and audited in access reviews. NIST 800-53 references it as AC-5, Separation of Duties.</Definition>

One row deserves a second look. A **non-zero access-review revocation rate is a healthy sign**, not a failure. If your reviews come back "100% completed, zero revocations" every quarter, the reviews are almost certainly being rubber-stamped. A real review, where managers actually look, produces some removals. Reporting revocation rate (and reviewer challenge rate) alongside completion percentage is what separates a governance program that works from one that just generates a compliance artifact.

## The one-slide executive dashboard

The discipline of "one slide, six tiles" forces you to decide what matters. If everything is on the dashboard, nothing is. Use RAG (red/amber/green) status, a trend arrow, and a one-line "so what" per tile. Close with a single ask.

Copy this mock and fill in your real numbers:

| Tile | Metric | Value | RAG | Trend | So what |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | MFA coverage | 91% | Amber | Up | 9% of users still password-only — our top residual auth risk |
| 2 | Terminations deprovisioned within 24h | 94% | Amber | Up | Closing toward 98% target; one HR-feed gap remains |
| 3 | Access reviews completed on time | 96% | Green | Flat | Governance cadence holding |
| 4 | Privileged accounts under PAM | 78% | Red | Up | 22% of admin access still standing — board-level exposure |
| 5 | Open SoD violations (aged &gt; 30d) | 4 | Amber | Down | Two require role redesign, not just revocation |
| 6 | Legacy-auth sign-ins (KRI) | -30% QoQ | Green | Down | Attack surface shrinking; on track to block by Q4 |

**The ask:** _[One sentence. Example: "Approve $X to complete PAM rollout (tile 4) — the largest remaining unmanaged risk."]_

Notice what this is not: it is not a list of how many tickets the team closed. Executives do not fund activity; they fund risk reduction. Every tile answers "so what for the business," and the slide ends by asking for exactly one thing.

## RFP design: getting the right tool, defensibly

When you move from "we should improve PAM" to "we are going to buy something," procurement begins. Three document types front-run that purchase, and they are not interchangeable:

- **RFI (Request for Information)** — you are still learning the market. Open-ended. "Tell us what your platform does." Use it early, when you are not yet ready to commit to requirements.
- **RFP (Request for Proposal)** — you know what you need and want competing proposals scored against it. This is where the weighted matrix lives.
- **RFQ (Request for Quote)** — you know *exactly* what you want and are comparing on price. Commoditized purchases.

For an IAM platform selection, you almost always want an RFP. Here is the anatomy:

1. **Background and objectives** — who you are, what problem you are solving, what success looks like.
2. **Scope** — what is in, what is explicitly out.
3. **Requirements** — split into **mandatory** (a "no" disqualifies) and **desirable** (scored, not pass/fail), each traced to a capability domain from section 01.
4. **Technical requirements** — connector/integration coverage, deployment model, scalability, the specifics.
5. **Security and compliance requirements** — vendor assurance artifacts (SOC 2 Type II, ISO/IEC 27001, and FedRAMP authorization for federal work), data residency, breach notification.
6. **SLAs** — uptime, support response, incident handling.
7. **Pricing template** — a fixed format so you can compare apples to apples (see the budget section's TCO framing — license + implementation + run).
8. **Implementation and support expectations** — onboarding, professional services, training.
9. **Evaluation methodology** — *disclosed to bidders.* You tell them how you will score, which keeps the process fair and defensible.

The single most important authoring skill: **write requirements as testable, weighted statements**, not vague wishes. "The system should be secure" is unscoreable. "The system shall support phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2/WebAuthn) for all administrative sign-ins" is testable — a vendor either demonstrates it or does not.

For the federal / VOSB angle (developed further in section 04): government procurement runs through the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) and SBA set-aside rules. Confirm any specific FAR clause or dollar threshold against the current regulation before you cite it in a real solicitation — those numbers change.

### RFP requirements template

| Req ID | Requirement (testable) | Mandatory / Desirable | Weight | How scored |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| R-01 | The system shall provision and deprovision accounts via SCIM to our top 10 SaaS apps | Mandatory | — | Pass/fail demo |
| R-02 | The system shall support FIDO2/WebAuthn phishing-resistant MFA for admin sign-ins | Mandatory | — | Pass/fail demo |
| R-03 | The system should automate access-review campaigns with reviewer delegation | Desirable | 15 | 1-5 demo rating |
| R-04 | The system should enforce SoD toxic-combination policies at request time | Desirable | 15 | 1-5 demo rating |
| R-05 | The vendor shall hold a current SOC 2 Type II report | Mandatory | — | Evidence review |
| R-06 | The system should provide JIT / time-bound privileged access | Desirable | 20 | 1-5 demo rating |

Fill in your own R-IDs against your real shortlist of needs. The weights on the desirable rows feed straight into the scoring matrix below — and they must be agreed *before* anyone sees a demo.

<Definition term="weighted scoring matrix">A vendor-selection tool where each evaluation criterion is assigned a weight (importance), each bidder is rated against each criterion (typically 1-5), and the weighted score is the sum of weight times rating across all criteria. It makes a selection defensible by quantifying fit against criteria everyone agreed to in advance, rather than relying on gut feel or the loudest voice in the room.</Definition>

### RFP weighted scoring matrix

This reuses the desirable requirements above as scoring rows. Weights are agreed up front; each vendor gets a 1-5 rating per criterion; weighted score = weight times rating. The worked example below shows the arithmetic — swap in your real criteria and ratings.

| Criterion | Weight | Vendor A score (1-5) | Vendor B score (1-5) | Weighted A | Weighted B |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Access-review automation (R-03) | 15 | 5 | 3 | 75 | 45 |
| SoD enforcement (R-04) | 15 | 4 | 2 | 60 | 30 |
| JIT privileged access (R-06) | 20 | 5 | 4 | 100 | 80 |
| Connector coverage (R-07) | 10 | 3 | 4 | 30 | 40 |
| Vendor stability / roadmap | 20 | 4 | 3 | 80 | 60 |
| Total cost of ownership (scored separately) | 20 | 2 | 5 | 40 | 100 |
| **Weighted total** | **100** | — | — | **385** | **355** |

A few defensibility rules baked into that table:

- **Score technical fit and cost separately.** Notice TCO is its own row, scored independently. A cheaper vendor (B) can win on cost while losing on capability — surfacing both lets the decision-makers weigh the trade-off honestly instead of letting price quietly dominate.
- **Use independent scorers** and have each document a rationale per score. Average them.
- **Agree the weights before the demos.** If cost should matter more, raise its weight up front — do not re-score capability downward after the fact to make your preferred vendor win. That after-the-fact thumb on the scale is exactly the bias the matrix exists to prevent.
