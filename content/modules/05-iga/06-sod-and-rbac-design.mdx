# SoD Modeling & RBAC Design

Every IGA program eventually collides with two design problems that decide whether the whole investment pays off or quietly rots into a compliance liability. The first is segregation of duties — encoding the rules about which combinations of access no single person is allowed to hold. The second is role design — deciding what a "role" actually means in your organization and keeping that model from collapsing under its own weight. Get these two right and your certifications mean something, your auditors stop circling, and a job change cleanly reshuffles access. Get them wrong and you end up with 4,000 roles for 1,200 people, a segregation-of-duties ruleset nobody trusts, and a recertification campaign that is theater. This section is about getting them right.

This is where the previous five sections converge. IGA fundamentals (section 01) gave us the access lifecycle and the RBAC-versus-ABAC distinction. SailPoint (section 02) gave us the two-tier role vocabulary — business roles versus IT roles. Now we put a designer's hands on those primitives.

## What it is

Two concepts anchor everything here. The first is the control principle. The second is the modeling discipline that implements it.

<Definition term="Segregation of Duties (SoD)">Also called separation of duties. The control principle that no single individual should be able to control all phases of a sensitive process. The canonical example is the maker-checker pattern: the person who creates a vendor record must not also be able to approve payments to that vendor, because together those two capabilities let one person create a fake vendor and pay it. NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5 control AC-5 frames the objective precisely: separation of duties addresses the potential for abuse of authorized privileges and helps reduce the risk of malevolent activity without collusion. By splitting a sensitive process across two or more people, you force any fraud to require collusion, which is far harder to sustain and far easier to detect. SoD is the control objective sitting behind SOX IT general controls and behind the access reviews IGA platforms run.</Definition>

The mechanics of how SoD is expressed in a system come down to one more idea — the thing the ruleset actually looks for.

<Definition term="toxic combination">A specific pair (or larger set) of entitlements or business functions that, when held together by a single identity, create an unacceptable SoD conflict. The classic banking pair is create-vendor and approve-payment; another is initiate-wire and release-wire. A toxic combination is not about any one permission being dangerous — each is legitimate on its own. The danger is the combination in one person's hands. An SoD ruleset is, at its core, a catalog of toxic combinations the organization has decided to police.</Definition>

The modeling side of the house is role-based access control, which you met in section 01. Here the focus shifts from what RBAC is to how you design it without it falling apart. Two opposing engineering approaches produce candidate roles, and most mature programs use both.

<Definition term="role mining">The bottom-up, data-driven discovery of candidate roles by analyzing the access people actually hold and clustering users who share the same entitlements. If 340 people in three branches all hold the same eight AD groups, those eight groups are a strong candidate for a single role. Role mining reads the existing access landscape and proposes structure that already implicitly exists. Its weakness is that it faithfully reproduces whatever mess is already there, including over-provisioning, so its output is a starting hypothesis, not a finished design. It contrasts with top-down role engineering, which designs roles from business processes and job functions rather than from observed data. Mature programs blend the two: top-down for the business meaning, bottom-up to validate it against reality.</Definition>

## Why it exists

SoD exists because access on its own does not commit fraud — combinations do. A purchasing clerk who can create vendors is doing their job. An accounts-payable specialist who can approve payments is doing theirs. The organization only has a problem when one person can do both, because then there is no second set of eyes between creating a payee and sending it money. This is why SoD is the structural backbone of SOX IT general controls: the entire point of Sarbanes-Oxley access controls is to demonstrate to an auditor that the people who can move money are not the same people who can authorize moving it, and that the system enforces that boundary rather than relying on goodwill.

Role design exists for a quieter but equally important reason: scale and auditability. In a small shop you can assign entitlements one at a time and remember why each one exists. In an enterprise with 1,200 employees and tens of thousands of entitlements scattered across AD, SAP, Salesforce, and forty other systems, per-entitlement assignment is unmanageable and unauditable. RBAC exists to compress that complexity into a layer a human can reason about: instead of asking "should this person have these 47 specific entitlements," you ask "should this person be a Branch Teller," and the role carries the entitlements. That compression is the entire value proposition of RBAC — and it is exactly what role explosion destroys.

<Definition term="role explosion">The failure mode where fine-grained, per-exception roles proliferate until there are nearly as many roles as users — or more roles than users. When that happens, RBAC has stopped compressing complexity and started adding to it. A reviewer looking at "1,400 distinct roles" learns nothing; the role names no longer map to recognizable job functions, and certifying role membership becomes meaningless rubber-stamping. Role explosion is caused by over-specific roles (one role per tiny variation), one-role-per-application sprawl, minting a new role for every one-off access request, and never retiring roles that outlived their purpose. It is the single most common reason RBAC programs fail to deliver the auditability they promised.</Definition>

When the business genuinely needs to violate an SoD rule, deleting the rule is never the answer. The framework has a sanctioned escape hatch.

<Definition term="mitigating control">Also called a compensating control. When the business has a legitimate, unavoidable need for an identity to hold a toxic combination — for example, a single controller at a small branch who must both create and approve during a year-end crunch — the SoD framework does not simply delete the rule. Instead it permits the conflict as a documented, approved, time-bound exception, paired with additional oversight: extra logging, an independent after-the-fact review of every transaction the conflicted user touches, and a defined expiration. The mitigating control preserves the control intent (accountability and detectability) even though the preventive barrier has been relaxed. This is the correct, auditable way to say yes to a conflict, versus the wrong way, which is silently turning the rule off.</Definition>

## How it works under the hood

### Modeling an SoD ruleset

An SoD ruleset is a catalog of conflicts expressed at some level of granularity. The first and most consequential design decision is which level: function-level or raw-entitlement-level.

A **function-level** ruleset describes conflicts in business terms — "Create Vendor" conflicts with "Approve Payment." Each business function is then mapped to the underlying entitlements that grant it (the transaction codes, the AD groups, the SaaS permissions). This is more durable: when an application changes the technical permission that grants "Approve Payment," you update one mapping rather than rewriting every conflict rule. It is also far more legible to the business owners who must actually decide what conflicts. A **raw-entitlement-level** ruleset names specific entitlements directly. It is faster to stand up for a single system but brittle and unreadable across many — and SoD conflicts very often span systems (create the vendor in the ERP, approve the payment in the banking platform), which is exactly why NIST AC-5 notes that conflicts can span systems and application domains and must be reasoned about across the whole estate, not one system at a time.

Once you have the ruleset, it is enforced in three distinct modes, and a serious program uses all three:

- **Preventive enforcement** blocks the conflict before access is ever granted. When a user requests access that, combined with what they already hold, would create a toxic combination, the request is stopped (or routed for exception handling) at request time. This is the gold standard because the conflict never exists.
- **Detective enforcement** finds conflicts that already exist. A certification campaign or a scheduled SoD scan reads current access and flags every identity holding a toxic combination. Detective controls are essential because access predates the ruleset, because preventive checks have gaps, and because off-platform changes happen.
- **Mitigating (compensating) controls** are how you handle the conflicts that must be allowed. Rather than removing the rule, you record an approved, time-bound exception with extra monitoring and independent review, as defined above.

The reference implementation of an industrial-strength SoD engine is **SAP GRC Access Control**, whose Access Risk Analysis (ARA) component scans user roles and profiles across the SAP landscape against a predefined ruleset of SoD conflicts and critical-access risks. SAP ships a standard best-practice ruleset of conflicting transaction-code combinations that organizations tailor to their own processes. The reason this matters for an IGA designer is that the broad IGA platforms — SailPoint, Saviynt — either integrate with SAP GRC to consume its ARA results or replicate the same ruleset concept natively so they can enforce SoD across systems beyond SAP. The vocabulary you learn from the SAP world (rulesets, functions, risks, conflicting actions) is the lingua franca of SoD everywhere.

<WarStory title="Four Thousand Roles for Twelve Hundred People">
A regional bank, roughly 1,200 employees, brought in an IGA platform to clean up access and pass its next SOX audit. Eighteen months later the external auditors delivered a finding that stopped the program cold: the bank had 4,000 distinct roles defined for 1,200 employees, and role membership was, in the auditor's words, "no longer evidentiary." A role called Teller_Branch_0412_PM_Drive_Through told you nothing useful; there were dozens of near-identical teller roles differing by branch, shift, and one or two entitlements. The recertification campaign that was supposed to prove access was appropriate had become a wall of opaque role names that managers approved in bulk because no human could meaningfully evaluate 4,000 roles.

How it happened was mundane and entirely typical. Every time someone needed a slightly different slice of access — a teller who also handled safe-deposit, a loan officer who covered two branches — the implementation team minted a new role rather than composing existing ones or using an exception path. One role per application per minor variation. No role was ever retired. Role mining had been run once at the start and its raw output (which faithfully reproduced years of accumulated over-provisioning) was promoted straight to production roles without any top-down consolidation. The platform was working exactly as configured. The configuration was the problem.

The remediation took the better part of a year and followed the two-tier model deliberately. The team defined a small set of broad business roles aligned to actual job functions — Teller, Senior Teller, Branch Manager, Loan Officer, maybe forty in total — modeled on what a person is. Underneath, they built narrow IT/technical roles that bundled the actual entitlements, modeled on what a person gets, and had the business roles require the appropriate IT roles. The branch-and-shift variations that had been minting roles were moved to attribute-based assignment: a teller's branch is an attribute, not a role, so one Teller business role plus a branch attribute replaced sixty branch-specific teller roles. Genuinely one-off needs were routed to a time-bound exception path instead of a permanent role. Finally — and this was the part the auditors cared about most — they put roles themselves under governance: every role got an owner, and roles were certified and pruned on a schedule so the explosion could not silently rebuild.

The bank went from 4,000 roles to roughly 350, and the next certification campaign produced reviewers who could actually read what they were approving. The lesson is not that role mining is bad or that the tool was wrong. The lesson is that RBAC's only real product is auditability, and a role model that a reviewer cannot reason about has destroyed the thing it was bought to provide. In a SaaS-first organization the same failure shows up as per-app, per-permission groups in the IdP; in a defense or healthcare environment it shows up as position-specific roles that fragment by unit and shift. The shape differs; the disease is identical.
</WarStory>

### Role engineering: top-down, bottom-up, and the two-tier model

Role design is the discipline that prevents the war story above. Two engineering directions feed it. **Top-down role engineering** starts from the business: interview department heads, map job functions to the access each function requires, and design roles that mirror the org chart and the work people actually do. It produces business-meaningful roles but can miss the messy reality of access that has accumulated outside any tidy job description. **Bottom-up role mining** (defined above) starts from the data, clustering users by shared entitlements to surface candidate roles that already exist implicitly. It captures reality but reproduces its flaws. Hybrid role mining — top-down to define the intended structure, bottom-up to validate and refine it against actual access — is what mature programs do, and it is the explicit recommendation in essentially every serious role-engineering methodology.

The structural answer to role explosion is the **two-tier role model**, the same business-role-versus-IT-role split SailPoint formalizes (section 02). Broad **business roles** model what a person is — Branch Teller, Loan Officer — and there should be relatively few of them, mapping cleanly to recognizable job functions. Narrow **IT/technical roles** model what a person gets — the actual bundles of entitlements — and a business role "requires" or "permits" the appropriate IT roles. The payoff is twofold. First, a reviewer certifying a business role is evaluating a comprehensible statement ("is this person a Branch Teller?") rather than an opaque entitlement dump. Second, when a person changes jobs (the Mover event), you swap their business role and the IT roles recompute, instead of hand-editing dozens of entitlements. The two tiers also let you separate the **birthright** roles everyone in a job gets automatically from the **requestable** roles a person asks for and an approver grants — a distinction that keeps the automatic grants minimal and least-privilege.

<ProTip>
Never solve a one-off access need by minting a new role. The single fastest way to cause role explosion is to treat "this one person needs this one extra thing" as a role-modeling problem. It is not — it is an exception. When someone needs access that does not fit an existing role, route it through a requestable, approved, time-bound exception path (an access request with an approver, or an attribute-driven assignment if the need is really a property of the person), and leave the role model alone. A role should represent a pattern that recurs across many people and persists over time. If exactly one person will ever hold a "role," it is not a role — it is an exception wearing a role's costume, and ten thousand of those costumes are how you end up with more roles than employees.
</ProTip>

### RBAC versus ABAC in design

Section 01 introduced RBAC and ABAC as access-control models; the design question here is sharper: for a given access need, do you model it as a role or as an attribute policy? The decision rule is durable and worth memorizing. Use a **role** when the access is stable, aligned to a recognizable org or job structure, and needs to be auditable as a unit — the kind of access a manager can attest to by name. Use an **attribute (ABAC) policy** when the access is context-dependent, dynamic, or high-cardinality — when modeling it as roles would require minting a role for every combination of values. NIST SP 800-162 (Guide to ABAC) is the standard reference for the attribute-based approach, and its core idea is that access is computed at evaluation time from subject, resource, and environment attributes against a policy, rather than pre-assigned through a role.

The high-cardinality case is the one that breaks RBAC and rescues ABAC. If a teller's access depends on branch, and there are sixty branches, you do not want sixty teller roles — you want one Teller role and a branch attribute that an ABAC policy reads to scope access to the right branch's resources. The attribute carries the variation; the role carries the job. This is precisely the substitution the war story bank made to collapse its teller roles. The broad industry framing for this convergence — RBAC for coarse, stable, auditable access plus attribute-driven policy for the dynamic dimension — is sometimes marketed as policy-based access control (PBAC), but the underlying engineering is the same hybrid the best programs have used for years: roles where roles make sense, attributes where they do not.

### A practical design method

Putting it together, a defensible role-and-SoD design program runs in a repeatable sequence:

1. **Inventory entitlements.** Aggregate accounts and entitlements from every in-scope source so you know the universe of grantable things you are modeling against (the aggregation/correlation work from section 02).
2. **Mine and interview for candidate roles.** Run role mining for the bottom-up view and interview business owners for the top-down view; reconcile the two into candidate roles.
3. **Define a two-tier role model.** Establish broad business roles and narrow IT/technical roles, separating birthright from requestable, and pushing high-cardinality variation to attributes.
4. **Write the SoD ruleset with the business.** Define toxic combinations at the function level, with the business owners who actually understand which combinations are dangerous; map functions to entitlements.
5. **Wire enforcement both ways.** Put preventive SoD checks into the access-request flow so conflicts are caught before grant, and put detective SoD scans into certification campaigns so existing conflicts surface and get remediated or mitigated.
6. **Govern the roles themselves.** Give every role an owner, certify and prune roles on a schedule, and route one-off needs to exceptions — so the model stays meaningful and role explosion cannot silently rebuild.
