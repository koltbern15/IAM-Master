# CrowdStrike Falcon Identity & Semperis

Section 02 covered Microsoft's native answer to identity attacks — Defender for Identity, included in the E5 stack, wired deep into Defender XDR and Entra ID Identity Protection. Native and integrated is a real advantage. But "native" is not the same as "complete," and the moment you operate Active Directory in anger you discover two gaps that the Microsoft-native tooling does not fully close.

The first gap is *real-time enforcement at the moment of authentication* with endpoint-to-identity correlation — the ability to block a Pass-the-Hash or Golden Ticket lateral move while it is happening, not just raise an alert that a SOC analyst triages an hour later. The second, larger gap is *recovery*. Detection tells you the forest was poisoned. It does not rebuild the forest. After a destructive attack — ransomware that encrypted your domain controllers, a wiper, a Golden-Ticket-compromised forest where you no longer trust the `krbtgt` key — you are no longer in the detection business. You are in the recovery business, and that is a different discipline with different tools.

This section covers the two best-known products that live in those gaps: **CrowdStrike Falcon Identity Protection** (best-of-breed real-time identity threat detection and enforcement, correlated with the endpoint) and **Semperis** (AD-specific change detection plus the only credible *automated forest recovery* story on the market). Both belong to a product category that Gartner named ITDR.

## What it is — the ITDR category

<Definition term="Identity Threat Detection and Response (ITDR)">A security product category, named by Gartner, focused on detecting and responding to attacks that target identity infrastructure itself — Active Directory, Entra ID, and the credentials, tickets, and trust material they manage. ITDR sits between EDR/XDR (which watch endpoints) and SIEM (which aggregates and correlates broad enterprise logs) by specializing in the identity layer: misconfigurations, risky delegations, credential theft, ticket forgery, and dangerous directory changes. The premise is that identity is now the primary attack surface, and a tool purpose-built for it catches what a general-purpose endpoint or log tool misses.</Definition>

The category exists because identity became the perimeter. Once a workforce went hybrid and SaaS-first, the attacker's path to your crown jewels stopped running through the network firewall and started running through a stolen token, a phished consent, or a forged Kerberos ticket. EDR watches the box. SIEM watches the logs. Neither was designed to understand the semantics of Active Directory — that a particular ACL grants `DCSync` rights, that a service account just authenticated from a host it has never touched, that someone disabled Kerberos pre-authentication on an account overnight. ITDR was designed for exactly that.

It is worth being precise about how these tools relate rather than compete:

- **EDR / XDR** (CrowdStrike Falcon Insight, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint) watch process execution, file activity, and memory on endpoints and servers.
- **SIEM** (Microsoft Sentinel, Splunk) ingest logs from everything and let you correlate and hunt — the subject of section 04.
- **ITDR** (CrowdStrike Falcon Identity Protection, Semperis, Microsoft Defender for Identity) specialize in the identity directory and authentication layer.
- **PAM** (CyberArk, Delinea) vault and broker privileged credentials — adjacent, but a control, not a detector.

The honest framing for this whole section: native (MDI) versus best-of-breed real-time enforcement (CrowdStrike) versus AD-specific resilience and recovery (Semperis). They overlap, but each owns a center of gravity the others do not.

## Why it exists — the gaps native tooling leaves

Microsoft Defender for Identity is genuinely good at detection, and if you live entirely in the Microsoft estate it may be all you need. Two pressures drive organizations to add a best-of-breed ITDR layer on top.

**Real-time blocking, not just alerting.** MDI's strength is detection and posture; its response actions (disable user, force password reset) are remediation after a detection. CrowdStrike's pitch is that it can sit *inline* with the authentication itself and make a block-or-step-up decision at the moment of sign-in — stopping the lateral move before it completes rather than alerting after.

**The recovery gap.** This is the one that keeps CISOs awake. You can have flawless detection and still be ruined. When ransomware encrypts every domain controller, or an attacker who held Domain Admin has been in your forest for months and you can no longer trust *anything* in it, the question is no longer "did we detect it" but "how do we stand up a clean, trustworthy forest." Microsoft's own answer to that is a long, manual runbook — and that runbook is where Semperis built a business.

<ProTip>
You can detect Golden Tickets all day. If you cannot *recover the forest*, you have not solved the problem. Detection without a tested recovery path is an incomplete program — the attacker only has to win once, and a trust-level compromise of `krbtgt` or a ransomed set of domain controllers cannot be cleaned by disabling accounts. Treat AD forest recovery as a first-class, rehearsed capability, not a wiki page nobody has read.
</ProTip>

## How it works under the hood — CrowdStrike Falcon Identity Protection

CrowdStrike Falcon Identity Protection (the product CrowdStrike has also marketed as Falcon Identity Threat Protection / Detection) is the identity module of the broader Falcon platform. The deployment model is the part most often described loosely, so be precise about it.

**Deployment model.** It is not purely agentless. Falcon Identity Protection uses a lightweight sensor connector deployed against the domain controllers so it has an inline, real-time view of authentication traffic, feeding the Falcon cloud platform. Because it sits at the DC, it sees the protocol-level authentication conversations — Kerberos, NTLM, LDAP/S, and (per CrowdStrike's documented protocol expansion) SMB to the DC — rather than relying solely on after-the-fact event logs. Treat the exact connector packaging and supported server versions as something to confirm against current CrowdStrike documentation before you scope a deployment; vendor module names and supported OS lists change.

**Continuous risk scoring.** The platform maintains a continuous identity risk score per account, derived from behavioral analytics: where the account normally authenticates from, which protocols it normally uses, whether it is over-privileged, whether it is a dormant or stale account suddenly active. That score is the input to policy.

<Definition term="risk-based authentication policy (CrowdStrike Falcon Identity)">A policy that consumes the continuous per-identity risk score and, at the moment of authentication, can allow, step up to multi-factor authentication, or block. Because Falcon Identity Protection can integrate inline with the sign-in flow — including, per CrowdStrike, support for Microsoft's External Authentication Method so it can act as an authentication checkpoint for Entra ID — the enforcement happens during the authentication rather than as a follow-up remediation. This is conceptually parallel to Entra ID Conditional Access risk-based policy <SC300Badge />, but driven by CrowdStrike's own risk engine and correlated with Falcon endpoint signals.</Definition>

**Threat detection coverage.** Falcon Identity Protection markets detection of the same attack families covered in section 01: Pass-the-Hash and other credential-reuse lateral movement, Golden Ticket usage, DCSync-style directory replication abuse, and anomalous service-account behavior. CrowdStrike states its real-time enforcement engine can block lateral-movement techniques such as Pass-the-Hash and Golden Ticket before they succeed. The specific detection and capability names should be verified against current CrowdStrike docs rather than asserted as fixed strings — phrase them as families, not product SKUs, unless you have the live datasheet in front of you.

**Endpoint-to-identity correlation.** The genuine differentiator over a standalone identity tool: because Falcon Identity Protection is part of the same platform as Falcon's endpoint detection, an identity anomaly can be correlated with what the endpoint sensor saw on the originating host. A suspicious authentication from a workstation that *also* just dumped LSASS (section 01, T1003.001) is a far higher-confidence signal than either event alone. That correlation across the endpoint and identity planes is the platform argument.

**Service-account visibility — the banking pain point.** A recurring, high-impact finding Falcon Identity Protection surfaces in real environments is the population of stale and over-privileged service accounts: accounts with non-expiring passwords, accounts that hold rights no current process needs, accounts authenticating in unexpected patterns. In a regulated environment like a bank these are audit findings waiting to happen and a favorite attacker target (recall Kerberoasting from section 01 feeds directly on service accounts with weak keys). Continuous identity-layer visibility is how you find them before an auditor — or an attacker — does.

## How it works under the hood — Semperis

Semperis approaches the same threat from the other end. CrowdStrike is built around real-time detection and enforcement at the authentication moment. Semperis is built around Active Directory itself — detecting dangerous *changes* to the directory, and, crucially, *recovering* the directory when prevention and detection have failed. Two products carry most of that story: Directory Services Protector and Active Directory Forest Recovery.

### Directory Services Protector (DSP)

Semperis Directory Services Protector provides continuous threat detection for Active Directory (and Entra ID), focused on dangerous changes and security posture. It tracks changes to the directory — including changes that bypass the security event log — surfaces indicators of exposure and indicators of compromise, and can automatically roll back malicious changes to their prior state. Confirm the exact auto-remediation feature name and Entra coverage against current Semperis documentation, since these capability names evolve.

<Definition term="Indicator of Exposure (IoE)">A configuration or state in Active Directory that an attacker could exploit, present *before* any active attack — for example a risky delegation, poor `krbtgt` password hygiene, accounts with Kerberos pre-authentication disabled, or dangerous ACLs granting replication rights. IoEs are about attack surface: closing them shrinks what an attacker can do. They contrast with an Indicator of Compromise (IoC), which is evidence that an attack has already happened. ITDR tooling such as Semperis DSP and Purple Knight scans for IoEs so you can fix the exposure before it is used against you.</Definition>

The DSP value proposition for a defender is twofold. First, change-tracking that does not depend solely on the Windows security log means an attacker who tampers with or disables auditing does not blind the detection. Second, the auto-undo capability turns a malicious change — a sudden grant of `DCSync` rights, an unexpected modification to a Tier-0 group — into a self-healing event rather than an incident that has to run all the way through a SOC ticket queue before it is reverted.

### Active Directory Forest Recovery (ADFR)

This is the capability that justifies the section. ADFR automates recovery of an entire AD forest to a clean, trustworthy state after a destructive attack — ransomware, a wiper, or a forest so deeply compromised at the trust level that you cannot believe anything inside it.

The baseline it automates is Microsoft's own guidance. Microsoft documents AD forest recovery as a long, manual runbook — widely cited as a 28-step process — that can take days or weeks to execute correctly under the pressure of a live incident, with ample room for a single mistake to leave the restored forest unhealthy. ADFR's pitch is to compress that to a largely automated workflow that restores a clean forest in hours rather than days.

<Definition term="Active Directory forest recovery">The process of rebuilding an entire AD forest to a known-good, trustworthy state after the directory itself has been compromised or destroyed — as opposed to recovering individual objects or a single domain controller. It is necessary when you can no longer trust the forest's integrity at the trust level: ransomware has encrypted the domain controllers, or an attacker held Domain Admin and may have planted persistence you cannot fully enumerate. Microsoft's native guidance is a long manual runbook (commonly cited as a 28-step process); Semperis ADFR automates that runbook to restore a clean forest in hours instead of days or weeks.</Definition>

Two design choices in ADFR are worth understanding because they show *why* automation matters here, not just *that* it is faster:

- **Malware-free recovery.** Semperis decouples Active Directory from the underlying operating system during recovery, so you do not restore the same OS image that the malware infected — which would simply re-infect the recovered forest. You bring AD back onto a clean OS. This addresses the classic trap of restoring from a backup that contains the very thing that compromised you.
- **Automation removes human error under pressure.** A 28-step manual runbook executed by an exhausted team at 3 a.m. during a ransomware event is a recipe for a missed step that leaves the forest broken. Automating the metadata cleanup, Global Catalog rebuild, and sequencing removes the most error-prone parts of the runbook from human hands.

## When you reach for which

A defender does not pick one of these and discard the others — they are largely complementary, and a mature program may run all three. But each has a center of gravity, and matching the gravity to the problem is the skill.

- **Microsoft Defender for Identity** — reach for it first if you are Microsoft-centric. It is native, included in Microsoft 365 E5 / E5 Security, and wired deep into Defender XDR and the Entra ID Identity Protection risk signal that feeds risk-based Conditional Access <SC300Badge />. Lowest friction, strongest integration, no extra vendor.
- **CrowdStrike Falcon Identity Protection** — reach for it when you want best-of-breed *real-time enforcement* at the authentication moment and *endpoint-to-identity correlation*, especially if you already run Falcon on your endpoints. Its differentiator is inline blocking and the cross-plane correlation a Microsoft-only stack cannot match in the same way.
- **Semperis** — reach for it for AD-specific *change detection that survives log tampering* and, above all, for *recovery*. Semperis owns the recovery story that neither MDI nor Falcon Identity owns. If your nightmare is "the forest is ransomed and we cannot trust anything in it," Semperis ADFR is the answer to that specific nightmare.

## War story

<WarStory title="The Forest That Came Back in Hours, Not Weeks">
A regional manufacturer — roughly 3,000 employees, a single AD forest with eight domain controllers across two data centers — got hit with a ransomware crew that did its homework. They did not just encrypt file servers. They had spent weeks inside the network, escalated to Domain Admin, and on the night of the detonation they encrypted every domain controller in both data centers along with the backup server that held the system-state backups. The forest was gone. Every authentication in the company depended on it, and it was a pile of encrypted disks.

The first instinct was to restore from the most recent system-state backup. That instinct was wrong twice over. The accessible backups had been encrypted along with everything else, and even the older offline copy carried the same compromised OS image the attackers had used to live in the environment for weeks — restoring it would have brought the attackers' persistence right back with the directory. This is the trap: a system-state restore recovers the malware along with the forest.

What saved them was that, six months earlier, after a Purple Knight assessment had lit up a wall of red indicators of exposure, the infrastructure lead had pushed through budget for Semperis — DSP for detection and, more importantly, ADFR with backups stored in cloud blob storage well outside the blast radius of the on-prem ransomware. Those forest backups were untouched.

The recovery ran the ADFR workflow: restore the forest onto clean operating systems decoupled from the infected images, rebuild the Global Catalog, clean up the metadata of the dead domain controllers, and bring the forest back without dragging the malware along. What Microsoft's manual 28-step runbook would have taken an exhausted team days or weeks to execute by hand — with every manual step a chance to leave the forest unhealthy — ran in hours. Authentication came back the same day. The lesson the infrastructure lead repeats to anyone who will listen: they did not get lucky in the recovery. They got lucky six months earlier, when they treated forest recovery as a capability to buy and rehearse rather than a wiki page to write later. Detection would have told them the forest was poisoned. Only the recovery capability brought it back.
</WarStory>
