# CIEM: Cloud Infrastructure Entitlement Management

The four sections before this one taught you three cloud entitlement engines and how to govern them as one. This section is about what happens after the policies are written and the workloads are running: entitlements drift, permissions accumulate, and the gap between what an identity *can* do and what it *actually does* widens every quarter. CIEM is the discipline — and the tooling category — that measures that gap and closes it.

## What it is

<Definition term="CIEM (Cloud Infrastructure Entitlement Management)">A category of identity-centric tooling that discovers, visualizes, and right-sizes cloud entitlements — the answer to who, or what, can do what across IaaS and PaaS — with the goal of enforcing least privilege at cloud scale. CIEM normalizes the very different permission models of AWS, Azure, and GCP into one view, analyzes actual usage from the cloud's activity logs, and recommends or automatically generates scoped-down policies. Gartner introduced the term in 2020.</Definition>

The shortest way to describe CIEM is "least privilege, automated, for the cloud." Every section of this module so far has assumed that someone authored the right policy — an SCP that filters correctly, a custom role with exactly the actions a workload needs, a deny policy on the right resource. In a small estate you can author those by hand and review them by reading. In a real cloud estate, with tens of thousands of identities and hundreds of services, each with hundreds of granular actions, no human can hold the permission graph in their head. CIEM is the tooling that holds it for you.

A CIEM platform connects to your cloud accounts read-only, ingests both the **granted** permissions (the policies, role assignments, and bindings you authored) and the **used** permissions (what those identities actually did, pulled from CloudTrail, Azure Activity Log, and GCP Cloud Audit Logs), and computes the difference. That difference — permissions granted but never exercised — is the over-privilege you want to remove. The platform then ranks identities by how over-privileged and how risky they are, recommends a trimmed-down policy for each, and in mature deployments can generate and even apply the scoped policy for you.

Crucially, CIEM is identity-centric, not resource-centric. It is not asking "is this S3 bucket public?" (that is CSPM's question). It is asking "this service account can delete every bucket in the project but has only ever read one — why does it still hold `storage.admin`?" The unit of analysis is the entitlement attached to an identity, and the headline target is the **machine identity**, because machine identities are where the over-privilege hides.

## Why it exists

CIEM exists because of a specific, measurable problem: the **permissions gap**, and the explosion of identities that gap rides on.

Start with the identity explosion. In cloud environments, non-human identities — service accounts, roles assumed by workloads, managed identities, API keys, CI/CD pipeline credentials — vastly outnumber human users. Recent identity-security reporting puts the ratio anywhere from dozens-to-one in a typical enterprise to many thousands-to-one in cloud-native estates; the exact multiple varies wildly by methodology and environment, but every credible source agrees on the direction: machine identities dominate, and they are growing far faster than human ones. These identities are created by automation, rarely reviewed by anyone, and almost never decommissioned.

<ProTip>The single most important thing to internalize about CIEM is *who* it is really for. Machine and workload identities outnumber humans in the cloud by a large multiple — reports range from dozens to many thousands to one depending on how cloud-native the estate is — and they accumulate far more permissions than they ever use, with no one watching. Human IAM gets the attention (it is what auditors ask about), but the over-privileged service account nobody remembers creating is the bigger blast radius. CIEM is aimed at the machine identities precisely because that is where the unmanaged risk lives.</ProTip>

Now layer on the permissions gap. Every one of those identities tends to be granted broad permissions at creation — a developer reaches for `Contributor`, `roles/editor`, or a wildcard `s3:*` because it is faster than scoping precisely, and it makes the thing work today. Over time the identity uses a tiny fraction of what it was granted, but the grant never shrinks. Microsoft's CIEM, Entra Permissions Management, quantified this directly with the **Permissions Creep Index** (a metric worth knowing even though Microsoft retired the product itself in late 2025).

<Definition term="Permissions Creep Index (PCI)">A Microsoft Entra Permissions Management metric that scores the gap between the permissions an identity has been granted and the permissions it has actually used over a measurement window. A high index means the identity is carrying a large block of unused, high-risk permissions — a wide gap between granted and used, and therefore a large potential blast radius if the identity is compromised. The same granted-versus-used idea appears under other names in other CIEM tools; the PCI is simply Microsoft's branded version of it.</Definition>

The security argument is blast radius. An over-permissioned identity is a force multiplier for an attacker: compromise one service account with `storage.admin` it never needed, and the attacker inherits the ability to exfiltrate or destroy every bucket in the project, even though the legitimate workload only ever read one file. Least privilege shrinks that blast radius to the minimum the workload actually requires. CIEM is the practical, continuous way to enforce least privilege when the number of identities and actions is far past what manual review can cover. This is exactly **NIST SP 800-53 AC-6 (Least Privilege)** operationalized for the cloud — the control says grant only the access required to perform assigned tasks; CIEM is the machinery that measures whether you are actually doing that and walks you back toward it when you are not.

## How it works under the hood

### The core capabilities

A CIEM platform is a loop of five capabilities. Map each one to the native analyzers you already met earlier in this module — CIEM tools generalize across all three clouds what those native tools do one cloud at a time.

- **Multi-cloud entitlement discovery.** Connect to every account, subscription, and project, read the policies and assignments, and normalize three incompatible permission models into one entitlement graph. This normalization is the hard part and the main reason you buy a tool instead of writing scripts: an AWS identity-based policy, an Azure role assignment at a scope, and a GCP policy binding are structurally nothing alike, yet CIEM has to render them as comparable entitlements.
- **Right-sizing recommendations.** Compare granted versus used by analyzing actual activity from CloudTrail, Azure Activity Log, and GCP Cloud Audit Logs, then recommend trimming the unused permissions — frequently generating a least-privilege policy you can apply. This is the same idea as **AWS IAM Access Analyzer** unused-access findings and policy generation (see Module 7 -> AWS IAM) and the **GCP IAM Recommender** (see Module 7 -> GCP IAM), unified across clouds.
- **Anomaly and threat detection.** Watch identity activity for behavior that does not fit the baseline — a service account that suddenly assumes a role it never has, a human who starts touching production at 3am from a new region.
- **Continuous monitoring and alerting.** Flag newly created high-risk grants the moment they appear, so the permissions gap does not silently re-widen after you close it.
- **Remediation.** Auto-generate the scoped-down policy, and in mature deployments apply it through an approval workflow or infrastructure-as-code pull request, so right-sizing is not a one-time cleanup but a standing control.

The native cloud analyzers are the free floor here. **AWS IAM Access Analyzer** (unused access, policy generation from CloudTrail) and the **GCP IAM Recommender** (excess-permission recommendations from usage) each surface excess permissions within their own cloud at no cost. On the Azure side, Microsoft's dedicated CIEM — **Entra Permissions Management** — played this role until Microsoft retired it in November 2025, leaving native Azure without a direct free equivalent. CIEM platforms exist because a multi-cloud organization wants one normalized view, one risk ranking, and one remediation workflow across all three rather than three separate consoles producing three incomparable findings.

<ProTip>Before you buy a CIEM platform, turn on the native analyzers — they cost nothing and they prove the problem is real. AWS IAM Access Analyzer unused-access findings and GCP IAM Recommender will both immediately surface dormant roles, stale access keys, and never-exercised permissions. If those findings alone are eye-opening (they usually are), you have your business case for a cross-cloud tool. If your estate is genuinely single-cloud, the native analyzer may be all you ever need.</ProTip>

### The tools — name them and tell them apart

You should be able to name the category leaders and say what differentiates them.

- **Microsoft Entra Permissions Management** (formerly CloudKnox, which Microsoft acquired in 2021) was Microsoft's dedicated multi-cloud CIEM, covering AWS, Azure, and GCP — not just Azure — computing the Permissions Creep Index across all three and auto-generating least-privilege policies. Know it as the canonical example of pure-play CIEM and the origin of the PCI metric, but note that Microsoft stopped selling it on April 1, 2025 and retired it (discontinuing support) on November 1, 2025, steering customers to third-party CIEM. Treat it as a concept reference, not a tool you can deploy today.
- **Wiz** is a CNAPP (cloud-native application protection platform) with a strong CIEM capability built on an effective-permissions graph that spans cloud configuration and identity. Its signature is attack-path and toxic-combination analysis: not just *this identity is over-permissioned*, but *this over-permission plus that misconfiguration plus this exposed workload chains into a path to your crown-jewel data*. Note that Wiz is broader than pure CIEM — it bundles CSPM and workload protection (CWPP) alongside the entitlement analysis — so CIEM is one capability of the platform rather than its whole identity.
- Other CIEM-capable platforms worth recognizing by name: **Sonrai Security**, **CrowdStrike Falcon Cloud Security**, and **Palo Alto Prisma Cloud**. Treat these as the broader CNAPP/CIEM market; the native analyzers (Access Analyzer, IAM Recommender) remain the no-cost floor beneath all of them.

### The operating model — how a team actually uses CIEM

A CIEM deployment is a loop, not a project. The shape of the loop is the same regardless of vendor, and it maps directly onto least-privilege doctrine:

1. **Baseline.** Connect read-only to every account and build the current entitlement graph. Resist the urge to change anything yet — first you measure.
2. **Identify.** Rank identities by the granted-versus-used gap (Permissions Creep Index or equivalent) and by risk. The top of that list is almost always machine identities holding admin-tier grants they never exercise.
3. **Right-size.** Generate and apply least-privilege policies for the worst offenders, ideally through the same infrastructure-as-code pipeline that created the access, so the change is reviewed and reversible.
4. **Guardrail.** Put controls in place — the SCPs, Azure Policy, and Organization Policy guardrails from earlier sections — so the gap cannot silently re-open after you close it. Right-sizing without guardrails just resets the clock on permission creep.
5. **Monitor and review.** Continuously watch for new high-risk grants and feed the entitlement data into periodic access certifications. This is where CIEM hands off to IGA: the human-identity access review (joiner-mover-leaver, recertification) is IGA's job; CIEM supplies the cloud entitlement evidence and extends the same least-privilege rigor to the machine identities IGA traditionally ignores.

That handoff is the point worth holding onto for the exam and for real work: CIEM is the cloud-scale, machine-identity-aware complement to human-focused IGA, both serving the same NIST AC-6 least-privilege goal from different ends.

### CIEM versus the neighbors

CIEM is constantly confused with PAM, IGA, and CSPM because all four live in the security-tooling neighborhood and all four touch "access" in some sense. The distinctions are clean once you anchor each tool to its core question:

| Tool | Core question it answers | Primary subject | Where it lives in this course |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **CIEM** | How much access does each cloud identity have, and how much does it actually need? | Cloud entitlements, especially machine identities | This section |
| **PAM** | How do we vault, broker, and record use of privileged credentials and sessions? | Privileged accounts and their credentials/sessions | Module 4 (PAM) |
| **IGA** | Who should have access, granted through what lifecycle and recertified how? | Human identity lifecycle and certification | Module 5 (IGA) |
| **CSPM** | Are the cloud resources themselves configured securely? | Resource configuration (not identity permissions) | Cloud security posture |

Read the table as four different questions, not four overlapping products. PAM is about *controlling the use of* privileged access — vaulting the credential, brokering the session, recording the keystrokes. CIEM is about *how much access exists in the first place* and trimming it. IGA governs the *human* lifecycle and runs the recertification campaigns. CSPM ignores identity almost entirely and asks whether the storage bucket, security group, and database are configured safely. The fast mnemonic: **PAM controls privileged use, IGA governs human lifecycle, CSPM checks resource config, and CIEM right-sizes entitlements — especially for machines.**

## War story

<WarStory title="The 9,000 Service Accounts Nobody Could Vouch For">
A mid-sized fintech, multi-cloud after acquiring a competitor, ran its primary platform in AWS and inherited a large Azure estate from the acquisition. The security team thought their identity hygiene was solid: human access was federated through Okta, MFA everywhere, quarterly access reviews on the Okta groups. Auditors were happy with the human side.

Then they turned on a CIEM trial across both clouds, in read-only discovery mode, and let it baseline for two weeks. The number that stopped the room: just over 9,000 non-human identities across the two clouds — IAM roles, Azure service principals and managed identities, and a long tail of static access keys — against roughly 400 employees. A 22-to-1 ratio nobody had ever measured, because nobody had ever been asked to. The quarterly access reviews covered the 400 humans and exactly none of the 9,000 machines.

The right-sizing report was worse than the count. Hundreds of those identities carried admin-tier grants — `AdministratorAccess`, Azure `Contributor` or `Owner` at subscription scope — with a Permissions-Creep-style gap showing they had exercised a single-digit number of distinct actions in the entire 90-day log window. The standout was a CI/CD role from a long-dead pipeline: it still held `AdministratorAccess` in the production account, its access key had not been rotated in over two years, and CloudTrail showed it had not made a single API call in eight months. It was a dormant skeleton key to production that no review process was ever going to catch, because no review process was looking at machines.

What they did, in order, was the operating loop. They did not mass-revoke — that is how you cause a 2am outage when some quarterly batch job you forgot about suddenly cannot authenticate. Instead: baseline first; rank by the granted-versus-used gap; for the top offenders, generate the scoped-down policy from actual usage and apply it through their Terraform pipeline so every change was peer-reviewed and revertible; the dormant CI/CD role they disabled behind a one-week break-glass window and then deleted when nothing screamed. Then they put a guardrail in place — an SCP denying the creation of new long-lived access keys, pushing every new workload onto short-lived role credentials — so the key sprawl could not silently regrow.

The lesson is the one the ProTip leads with: the human IAM program was genuinely good, and it was guarding the smaller blast radius. The real exposure was 9,000 machines that no human-centric review would ever touch, found only when a tool was pointed at the entitlement graph and asked the granted-versus-used question. In a bank or a defense environment the moral is identical, only the stakes are higher: the over-privileged service account you have never heard of is, statistically, your largest standing risk, and CIEM is how you find it before an attacker does.
</WarStory>
